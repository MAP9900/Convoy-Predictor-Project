A Brief History of the Battle of the Atlantic and the Convoy System

The Battle of the Atlantic was the longest campaign of the war, lasting from September 3, 1939 to May 8, 1945. Often overlooked in its importance, this naval campaign kept the Atlantic corridor open and connected the Americas with Europe and North Africa. It carried a lifeline of Lend-Lease aid that supplemented the war‑torn economies of the United Kingdom, France, the Soviet Union, and other countries. If Germany had been capable of controlling the Atlantic sea lanes, it could have severed this connection and threatened the Allies’ ability to wage war. Such was the importance of the battle that Winston Churchill considered it “the only thing that ever really frightened [him] during the war [was]… the U‑boat peril” (Churchill 1949, 529). Had Germany’s U‑boats taken control of the seas, Hitler could have starved Britain into submission, bled the Soviet Union dry of raw materials and, and thwarted any hopes of an Allied invasion of the European mainland. Convoys, therefore, played an essential role in stopping an Axis victory. As the battle spanned the length of the entire war, it can be divided into seven periods, each characterized by a shifting locus of combat, the deployment of new tactics, and the overall effectiveness of the U‑boats.

  • Period I (September 1939 – May 1940)
  • Period II (June 1940 – March 1941)
  • Period III (April 1941 – December 1941)
  • Period IV (January 1942 – September 1942)
  • Period V (October 1942 – June 1943)
  • Period VI (July 1943 – May 1944)
  • Period VII (June 1944 – May 1945)

Period I (September 1939 – May 1940)

This period of the Second World War is often referred to as the “Phoney War” for the lack of Allied military engagements, a pattern reflected in the Atlantic. A limited number of German U‑boats based in Germany operated primarily around the British Isles. It was not until December 1939 and War Order No. 154 that Germany began to engage in unrestricted submarine warfare. Much of the early German effort focused on mine‑laying, often carried out by U‑boats. For the Royal Navy, the initial focus was enforcing a naval blockade of Germany and reviving the convoy system from the First World War. Limited offensive measures were taken against U‑boats during this period. The U‑boats’ small silhouette and a lack of effective weapons and tactics left them relatively free from attack. U‑boats that did attack tended to focus on independent merchant ships during daytime. The Norwegian Campaign and other early engagements revealed flaws in U‑boat torpedoes and tactics, however. Nazi leadership, also did not view the U-boats as a priority meaning low early war production rates.


Period II (June 1940 – March 1941)

With the fall of France in June 1940, the Battle of the Atlantic entered a new phase. The disappearance of the French Navy—the world’s fourth largest—forced the Royal Navy to stretch its forces even thinner. Construction of U‑boat bases in France began, reducing the transit distance to the Atlantic by about 450 miles (U‑boats were forced to pass around the British Isles rather than through the English Channel). The fall of Western Europe also meant the full U‑boat force could be directed against the United Kingdom and the Royal Navy. This era became known as the “Happy Time” to U‑boat crews as they enjoyed relatively uncontested success. The main focus of attacks was the western approaches to Great Britain. U‑boat tactics evolved: boats spread out to cover this vast area and spot convoys and independent ships. Once a target was located, other U‑boats were notified and assembled into packs to attack, often at night. These “wolf‑pack” attacks proved extremely deadly, and the Royal Navy and merchant shipping suffered some of their worst losses in September and October 1940. Luftwaffe aircraft operating from France and Kriegsmarine surface raiders further threatened shipping.


Period III (April 1941 – December 1941)

The disastrous losses of the previous months forced the Royal Navy to reintroduce Atlantic convoys, a legacy of the First World War. The first convoys were organized near Europe, then the primary operating area of U‑boats, leading to initial successes in blunting wolf‑pack attacks. Increased U‑boat losses pushed the Kriegsmarine to operate farther west, attempting to strike before convoys formed. In June 1941, full escort support was introduced, meaning convoys would be protected for the entire Atlantic crossing—thanks in part to ex‑American destroyers (from the “destroyers for bases” agreement) and Canadian‑built escorts that became available in this period. The United States began taking a more active role despite its neutrality, extending the Pan‑American Security Zone to just short of Iceland. In this zone American warships escorted convoys to deter U‑boat attacks and avoid provoking a formal declaration of war. The extension did, however, create hostile encounters between U‑boats and American warships prior to America’s formal entry into the war. Rapid expansion of the U‑boat force created issues with crew training and effectiveness, though these diminished as experience grew. Despite improved convoy defense, relatively few U‑boats were being sunk—only a temporary relief for the Allies.


Period IV (January 1942 – September 1942)

Adolf Hitler’s declaration of war against the United States at the end of 1941 changed the scope of the U‑boat campaign. It now encompassed the majority of the Atlantic, bringing the war to the American shore. The Kriegsmarine—despite having only five long‑range Type IX U‑boats at first— quickly began attacks off the American East Coast. U.S. naval command initially rejected the Royal Navy’s calls for coastal blackouts and East Coast convoys. Without blackouts and warship protection, U‑boats could easily spot merchantmen silhouetted against city lights, with little fear of retaliation. Heavy shipping losses followed, prompting criticism of the Navy; naval leaders argued the Army had the legal authority to enforce blackouts and that available surface forces were prioritized for troop transports and Lend‑Lease to the USSR or were committed to the Pacific. By May, more escort ships enabled convoys along the Eastern Seaboard, and the first U‑boat sinkings off American shores occurred. U‑boats reacted by pushing into the Caribbean and Gulf of Mexico, where ships were not yet sailing in convoys. As U‑boat losses mounted, the Kriegsmarine withdrew from the East Coast by summer, returning the battle to the Mid‑Atlantic. The vast distances and lack of resupply bases made East Coast patrols difficult and ultimately unsustainable despite early success against an unprepared America.


Period V (October 1942 – June 1943)

Back in the Mid‑Atlantic, U‑boats focused their attacks on the “air gap,” the region outside the range of Allied aircraft operating from North America or the United Kingdom. U‑boats returned to wolf‑pack attacks—often 10–15 submarines striking a single convoy. With many U‑boats at sea and a high volume of convoys, contacts were frequent. U‑boats would search for and tail convoys by day, waiting until night to launch attacks. These tactics aimed to overwhelm escorts and exploit vulnerabilities in the screen. They were highly effective at first—particularly in March 1943, when 82 ships were sunk in the Atlantic. Yet as merchant losses mounted, the U.S. shipbuilding industry reached full force, producing roughly 100 Liberty ships per month in 1943. U‑boat losses also increased, threatening the tonnage‑sunk‑per‑boat ratio. The growing number of escorts enabled more offensive Allied tactics. Support groups were formed that could detach during attacks to hunt and drive off U‑boats while leaving enough escorts with the convoy. Allied ships developed “hold‑down” tactics: shadowing submerged submarines until they were forced to surface for air or to recharge batteries. Technological advances—better sonar and radar, the cracking of Enigma, and new weapons like the Hedgehog mortar—further strengthened Allied capabilities. Finally, long‑range aircraft and escort carriers closed the air gap, forcing U‑boats to abandon the Mid‑Atlantic and return to European coastal waters.


Period VI (July 1943 – May 1944)

Operations in the North Atlantic became increasingly dangerous for U‑boats; the hunter had become the prey. Aircraft proved highly effective, and U‑boats lacked significant air‑defense weapons. This pushed them toward seemingly less‑defended regions in the central and southern Atlantic. With Italy’s surrender in September 1943, the Royal Navy was able to redeploy destroyers from the Mediterranean to bolster Atlantic defenses. Later that month, Kriegsmarine chief Karl Dönitz attempted to surprise the Allies with renewed wolf‑pack attacks in the North Atlantic. Although U‑boats saw initial success, later wolf‑pack operations suffered considerable losses and were abandoned. From this point on, U‑boats were again dispersed. Losses now exceeded the rate of new U‑boat production, and the intensifying air campaign against German industry made matters worse. New variants—including the hydrogen‑peroxide‑powered Elektroboote—were in development, but delays and over‑promises plagued the program. The first of the new Type XXI U‑boats was launched in April 1944; however, modest technological gains and low production meant little effect on stopping Allied shipping. As an Allied invasion loomed, U‑boats increasingly operated around mainland Europe as Germany sought to preserve its fleet. By May 1944, only about ten U‑boats were operating in the Atlantic. What had once posed an existential threat to Allied victory had been reduced to a nuisance in Allied logistics.


Period VII (June 1944 – May 1945)

The invasion of Normandy saw all U‑boats recalled from the Atlantic. Tactics turned purely defensive as Germany tried feverishly to stop the inevitable. All available U‑boats were sent to confront the invasion force; they sank eight of roughly 5,000 ships participating in the landings at the cost of thirteen U‑boats. Constant air patrols kept U‑boats from traveling on the surface, restricting speed and rendering any boat without a schnorkel (German for snorkel) largely ineffective. As Allied armies pushed through France, the U‑boat bases themselves were threatened. The major bases at Brest and Bordeaux were lost in August–September, pushing remaining U‑boats north to Norway. Notably, other French bases remained occupied until the end of the war, but their boats had been evacuated. The Allied priority was breaking out of France toward Germany, so a “bypass and contain” strategy was adopted for German holdouts to limit casualties and maintain momentum. The few remaining U‑boats in Norway were too far from Allied convoy lanes, limiting their attacks largely to British waters. Some expeditions to American and Canadian waters saw limited success but were insufficient to change the strategic picture. As the Red Army overran Baltic U‑boat bases, surviving submarines fled to Norway under heavy Allied air attack. The U‑boat force—like much of the German military—was reduced to disarray. The last engagement of the Battle of the Atlantic occurred on May 7–8, 1945, when U‑320 was sunk by the Royal Air Force. Germany’s surrender on May 8 marked the campaign’s end. The war’s longest campaign ended in an overwhelming Allied victory, owing largely to rapid technological advancement and the United States’ ability to mass‑produce commercial and military ships. Remaining U‑boats were either scuttled by their crews or fell into Allied hands. The Atlantic was, at last, free from the underwater threat.
